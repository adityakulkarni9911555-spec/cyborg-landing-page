The System

Three components.
One continuous loop.

Nothing about SYNAPSE works in isolation. Each part exists to keep the signal moving — out to the limb, and back again as sensation.

01 — Sensing

Neural Cuff

A spiral electrode array implanted around the residual nerve bundle. It reads raw efferent traffic — the pattern your brain already sends when you mean to move — without requiring you to learn a new one.

02 — Decoding

Cortex Bridge

A coin-sized processor worn at the socket. It classifies incoming signal against your personal motor library in real time and resolves it into a specific, continuous joint command.

03 — Actuating

Kinetic Limb

Seventeen independently driven joints translate the decoded command into motion, while embedded pressure sensors send texture and grip force back up through the same cuff.

How Signal Becomes Motion

Four stages.
Under fifteen milliseconds.

This is the same loop, every time you reach for something — it happens fast enough that it stops feeling like a device and starts feeling like a hand again.

01
Intent
A nerve fires the instant you think to move — the same efferent signal it always sent, whether or not there's still a hand on the other end of it.
02
Capture
The neural cuff's 128 electrodes pick up that raw pattern across the residual nerve bundle and pass it to the Cortex Bridge as a continuous stream.
03
Decode
The Bridge matches the pattern against your trained motor library — built during a two-week calibration period — and resolves it into a joint-level command in under 15ms.
04
Actuate & Return
The limb moves, and its pressure sensors immediately send grip force and texture back up the same pathway — so the next intent you form already accounts for what you're holding.
